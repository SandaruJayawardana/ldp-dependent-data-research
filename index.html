Local Differential Privacy Under Statistical Dependence

Privacy leakage, Information-theoretic privacy analysis, Dependency triad, and Coordinated perturbation

About Projects Publications Acknowledgments Contact

About

This research focuses on privacy-preserving data collection under Local Differential Privacy (LDP), especially for multidimensional and statistically dependent user data.

We first theoretically study the impact of attribute dependencies on privacy under Local Differential Privacy (LDP) and develop algorithms to quantify privacy leakage when the joint distributions of attributes are known.

Next, we introduce Dependency Triad (DT), a compact metric for characterizing attribute dependencies using potentially imperfect prior distributional knowledge, enabling the estimation of potential privacy leakage under LDP.

Building on these insights, we develop privacy-budget calibration algorithms that leverage prior distributional knowledge to achieve improved privacy-utility trade-offs. We further propose coordinated perturbation mechanisms that exploit dependency information to reduce unintended privacy leakage while preserving utility-relevant information.

The Hidden Cost of Correlation: Rethinking Privacy Leakage in Local Differential Privacy

Study on the impact of attribute correlation on LDP privacy leakage.

Abstract

Local differential privacy (LDP) has emerged as a promising paradigm for privacy-preserving data collection in distributed systems, where users contribute multi-dimensional records with potentially correlated attributes. Recent work has highlighted that correlation-induced privacy leakage (CPL) plays a critical role in shaping the privacy-utility trade-off under LDP, especially when correlations exist among attributes. Nevertheless, it remains unclear to what extent the prevailing assumptions and proposed solutions are valid and how significant CPL is in real-world data. To address this gap, we first perform a comprehensive statistical analysis of five widely used LDP mechanisms-GRR, RAPPOR, OUE, OLH and Exponential mechanism-to assess CPL across four real-world datasets. We identify that many primary assumptions and metrics in current approaches fall short of accurately characterising these leakages. Moreover, current studies have been limited to a set of pure LDP (i.e., δ=0) mechanisms. In response, we develop the first algorithmic framework to theoretically quantify CPL for any general approximated LDP ((ε,δ)-LDP) mechanism. We validate our theoretical results against empirical statistical results and provide a theoretical explanation for the observed statistical patterns. Finally, we propose two novel benchmarks to validate correlation analysis algorithms and evaluate the utility vs CPL of LDP mechanisms. Further, we demonstrate how these findings can be applied to achieve an efficient privacy-utility trade-off in real-world data governance.

Main Findings

  • We provide a theoretical analysis of correlation-induced privacy leakage (CPL) under (ε,δ)-LDP mechanisms, introducing two novel algorithm.
  • We propose two novel benchmarks: (i) A CPL analysis benchmark to assess and compare existing privacy analysis algorithms; and (ii) A privacy-utility benchmark that quantifies the trade-off between data utility and CPL exposure across LDP mechanisms.
  • We discover that CPL between two correlated attributes is asymmetric,.

Dependency Triad: A Metric to Quantify Dependencies Between Attributes for Local Differential Privacy

A framework for summarizing dependency structure and bounding correlation-induced privacy leakage.

Abstract

Collecting multidimensional user data is essential for extracting rich insights across various applications. Local Differential Privacy (LDP) has emerged as a de facto standard for mitigating privacy risks in such scenarios. A key challenge in privacy-preserving multidimensional data collection lies in inter-attribute dependencies, as they can inadvertently reveal correlated information and increase privacy vulnerabilities. Therefore, accurately measuring correlation-induced privacy leakage (CPL) is essential for privacy analysis and privacy-utility trade-off. However, existing CPL analysis solutions either require accurate prior knowledge or face scalability challenges for large numbers of attributes and high-cardinality attributes. These limit their practical applicability in real data.

To address this research gap, we propose a novel metric, ''Dependency Triad'' (DT), which summarizes the pairwise dependency information relevant to CPL using three parameters and yields a constant-time conservative estimator of pairwise CPL. DT explicitly models uncertainty in prior distributional knowledge through its parameters, thereby delivering robust leakage estimates. Moreover, its robustness to sparse distributions makes it particularly suitable for high-cardinality attributes, while the pairwise formulation serves as a tractable building block for assessing total leakage in multidimensional settings. Extensive experiments on synthetic and real datasets demonstrate that DT consistently estimates CPL across diverse dependency regimes and prior uncertainties, supporting trade-offs between privacy and utility, and thereby enabling reliable LDP deployments.

Main Findings

  • Attribute dependencies can amplify privacy leakage under LDP.
  • Correlation-induced leakage can be analyzed using information-theoretic measures.
  • Compact dependency parameters can support efficient leakage estimation.

CoP: Coordinated Perturbation for Controlled Disclosure Under Local Differential Privacy

A dependency-aware LDP mechanism for controlled disclosure under multidimensional data collection.

Abstract

Collecting multidimensional user data is essential for personalized services, yet it poses significant privacy risks. While privacy regulations like the GDPR and CPRA advocate for data minimization, attribute correlations can inadvertently amplify unintentional information disclosure, leading to correlation-induced information leakage (CIL). Although data collectors often possess rich prior knowledge of these correlations, existing Local Differential Privacy (LDP) mechanisms are inadequate for effectively leveraging this information to reduce CIL.

In this paper, we propose CoP, a coordinated perturbation mechanism designed to mitigate CIL in multidimensional data collection while preserving utility. Unlike traditional LDP approaches, CoP explicitly incorporates prior distribution knowledge to coordinate the perturbation process across attributes. By optimizing the perturbation strategy based on known correlations, CoP achieves a better privacy-utility trade-off. Extensive evaluations across both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate that CoP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art LDP mechanisms in reducing disclosure while preserving analytical accuracy.

Main Findings

  • Coordinated perturbation can reduce unintended leakage.
  • Prior dependency knowledge can improve the privacy-utility trade-off.
  • CoP provides formal LDP protection while mitigating correlation-based inference.
